    An author-readership problem3

    "I have just bought Scott Kelby's 'The Digital Photograohy Book', it has just arrived and it has gone as quickly into the bin. I'm sure it is scattered with useful information but (1) not much of it seems relevant to my needs as quite (not very) experienced a photographer, who has just brought digital and SLR photography together for the first time; and (2) especially because the way the book is written is appallingly jejune.
    I'm sorry, Mr Kelby, but such a manner of writing is deeply patronising and it gets in the way of whatever good technical stuff that you do have to tell us.
    Such a deployment of 'humour' strikes me as a particular problem in author-reader relationships. I can think of a well known series of 'how to' books that also uses humour to bridge a perceived gap between, on the one hand, the desire to take whatever subject it is that is in hand seriously, and, on the other hand, a fear of being seen to take anything seriously at all. Our social image seems to demand of us something like a reaction that goes: "Hey! Look! I take life lightly! So I've always gotta be foolin' aroun' a bit! Got it!" Yeh, that's right, now we are all in the same club of people who are (privately) trying to make a good job of something but (publicly) fear the consequences of admitting that.
    Every book is an author-reader exchange, and this is one kind of exchange that I refuse to participate in. With apologies.
    Paul Griffiths, France" by a Book Depository customer
