    Synopsis

    In 1914, Joey, a beautiful bay-red foal with a distinctive cross on his nose, is sold to the army and thrust into the midst of the war on the Western Front. With his officer, he charges toward the enemy, witnessing the horror of the battles in France. But even in the desolation of the trenches, Joey's courage touches the soldiers around him and he is able to find warmth and hope. But his heart aches for Albert, the farmer's son he left behind. Will he ever see his true master again?

    Children's Literature

    Joey, a beautiful red bay horse, has settled into his life on the farm and his friendship with thirteen-year-old Albert. When Albert's father who is in need of money for the upkeep of the farm sells Joey to a British cavalry unit, Joey finds himself being shipped to Europe where World War I has just begun. The reader sees the war through Joey's eyes. His losses and his triumphs are captivatingly detailed. Captain Nicholls, his first rider, dies in battle but not before he paints a portrait of Joey. It is that portrait hanging in a village hall that inspired this tale. Later, Joey and his rider are captured by the Germans and Joey becomes part of the team of horses that pull the ambulance wagons from the battle line. After that he enjoys some loving, tender care from a young girl and her grandfather. That soon ends when he is conscripted to pull a canon for the German army. The horror of the conditions in which the soldiers live is mirrored in the events of Joey's life. A meeting between an old German and a young British soldier over who will take Joey provides a thoughtful commentary on the futility of war. This is an eloquently told and absorbing friendship story. Horse lovers will be on the edge of their seats from the beginning to the end. This runner-up for the Whitbread Award goes far beyond the horse story genre. Morpurgo presents both a fine history lesson and an emotional tale.

    Great, Simple Storyby maggiesaunt

    Reader Rating:
    See Detailed Ratings

    September 30, 2009: I ordered this because the play -- originally from London -- is coming to Broadway this year and I wanted to "get a preview." It is distinctly English, and thus there is some cultural references that might be missed by some. I would mostly recommend it for younger readers, although I was fully absorbed by it and completed it in about four hours. It would be a nice ancillary or outside reading piece for a History class doing WWI. It presents a clear, and not always easy-to-hear story about both the horses and the men in WWI, what they endured and, perhaps more importantly, why they endured. A friend said it was "wiered to have a horse narrator" -- but don't we live in a world with talking mice and dancing pumpkins, Tom and Jerry and Roadrunner and Coyote??? And this horse is extremely literate. It is a simple book, but certainly not a child's book. I may have my Sophomores read it next summer -- it's certainly less formidable to them than SARUM... (which I also love and which the Brit Lit Honors class reads over the summer.)

    From the Publisher

    Joey is a warhorse, but he wasn't always. Once, he was a farm horse and a gentle boy named Albert was his master. Then World War I came storming through and everything changed. Albert's father sells Joey to the army where the beautiful, red-bay horse is trained to charge the enemy, drag heavy artillery, and carry wounded soldiers not much older than Albert off of battlefields. Amongst the clamoring of guns and slogging through the cold mud, Joey wonders if the war will ever end. And if it does, will he ever find Albert again?
