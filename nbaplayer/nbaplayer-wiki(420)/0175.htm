Juwan Antonio Howard (born February 7, 1973, in Chicago, Illinois) is an American professional basketball player for the Miami Heat of the National Basketball Association (NBA). The Heat are his eighth different NBA team. He was drafted fifth overall in the 1994 NBA Draft by the Washington Bullets. A former All-Star and All-NBA power forward, he also starred as an All-American on the Michigan Wolverines men's basketball team during the Fab Five years and, earlier, on the Chicago Vocational Career Academy team as a center. In addition to being an All-American in high school, he was an honors student.

Howard was a member of the Fab Five (along with Chris Webber, Jalen Rose, Jimmy King and Ray Jackson) that reached the finals of the 1992 and 1993 National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA) Men's Division I Basketball Championship as freshmen and sophomores while starting a combined 304 of a possible 350 games. Most Michigan Wolverines men's basketball records and accomplishments from 1992 to 1998 have been forfeited because of NCAA sanctions stemming from the University of Michigan basketball scandal. However, Howard's 1993–94 All-American season accomplishment has not been.[1]

He played six and a half seasons for the Bullets/Wizard franchise and has played three full seasons for the Houston Rockets. He has not played more than two seasons for any other teams. During his rookie year as a professional, he became the first player to graduate on time with his class after leaving college early to play in the NBA. After one season as an All-Rookie player and a second as an All-NBA performer, he became the first NBA player to sign a $100 million contract. Although he continued to be a productive starter, he never again performed at an All-Star level. Near the end of his contract, he was traded at the NBA trade deadline twice to make salary cap room. In 2010, he is entering his 17th NBA season, including five during which his team reached the playoffs. During his first 5 seasons in the NBA, he averaged 19.3 points per game, but has only averaged 17 points in three of his seasons since. The 2005–06 NBA season was the most recent in which he was a starter in more than half of the games in which he played and averaged more than 30 minutes per game. Howard has developed a reputation as a humanitarian for his civic commitment.

[edit] Early life and high school

Overview

Howard's single mother was 17 when he was born and did not want to raise her child. Howard was brought up by his maternal grandmother, Jannie Mae Howard, who adopted him. He grew up in a three-bedroom apartment on 69th Street on the South Side of Chicago.[2] His biological father, Leroy Watson, Jr., wanted to name him Howard Leroy III, but his grandmother decided otherwise.[3]

Howard attended Chicago Vocational Career Academy, where he was named a 1991 All-American basketball player by Parade magazine and won McDonald's All American honors. He was also chosen for the National Honor Society.[3][4][5] During recruiting visits by college coaches such as Lou Henson, Joey Meyer and Fisher, Jannie Mae Howard did most of the questioning.[3] His grandmother died a few hours after he announced that he would attend Michigan.[2] After her fatal heart attack, Howard moved in with his high-school coach, Richard Cook.[3] Howard has no siblings and is not close to his biological parents.[6]

Sophomore year

At the start of his sophomore year in 1988, Howard was 15 years old and already expected to be a 1991 blue chip recruit, highly prized by college basketball coaches.[7] He was regarded as one of the best sophomores in the Chicago metropolitan area.[8] The Chicago Public School League coaches choose an annual roster of the best performers each season. He was a second-team selection and the only sophomore named to the 20-man 1988–89 All-Chicago Public School League team.[9][10]

The summer after his sophomore year, the 6-foot-8-inch (2.03 m) center attended the Nike Academic Betterment and Career Development (ABCD) camp, which was held annually in Princeton, New Jersey, during the late 1980s.[11] There he was matched against the 7-foot-4-inch (2.24 m) Shawn Bradley. At this camp, Howard established himself as one of the best junior-year big men (tall basketball players) in the country despite having his shots blocked several times by the much taller Bradley.[12] He was involved in controversy for receiving a second pair of sneakers at the camp.[13] Howard explained the situation as follows: "I was issued a pair of shoes at the beginning of camp. But they tore. I was issued another pair. Then a counselor gave me another pair. I thought it was a joke. But somebody saw me walk out with a shoebox and thought I had stolen some shoes." Under pressure from camp officials, on three occasions Howard denied he had taken the extra pair of shoes. Finally, as a disciplinary measure, he was shipped home on the last day of the six-day camp. Howard said. "I was so scared, I threw the shoes out of a window in my dormitory."[13] He also participated in the Bill Cronauer (B/C) camp in Rensselaer, Indiana, which was attended by more than 100 college coaches. During the camp, he became ranked as one of the top 10 underclassmen in the country.[13][14] Howard attended other camps that summer and had a goal of surpassing Deon Thomas, who was the reigning Chicago Tribune basketball player of the year,[15] as the best big man in the state.[13] By the time he ended his college career in 1994, Howard was drafted a full round ahead of Thomas, which marked Howard's achievement of his goal.[16]

Junior year

As Howard and Tom Kleinschmidt entered their junior years, some sources listed Kleinschmidt as the number one junior in the state.[17] Others considered Howard the top prospect.[18] Howard was said to be leaning toward playing for DePaul or, because of his admiration for Thomas, for the Illinois Fighting Illini.[18] By the end of his junior year, league coaches named him to the first-team All-Chicago Public School League.[19] He was selected to the Chicago Sun-Times All-Area team as well as the Class-AA All-State team and established himself as the top Chicago-area junior ahead of Kleinschmidt.[20][21][22] He had a sub-par performance against King High School in the Chicago Public School League semifinals,[23] but the Chicago Tribune named him to its All-State second team.[24]

Howard was also an honors student.[23] After his junior year, he was one of 10 Illinois players invited back to the Nike All-American Camp at Princeton. Others invited included Kleinschmidt, Donnie Boyce, William Gates, Billy Taylor, Rashard Griffith and Howard Nathan.[25] Although Howard was perceived as one of the top Chicago prospects at that time, the best Chicago-area prospect was Glenn Robinson of Gary, Indiana.[26] By this time, Howard had eliminated DePaul from consideration since Deryl Cunningham, another Chicago-area all-star who might have otherwise convinced Howard to stay in Chicago, had transferred to Kansas State. He was considering Michigan, Michigan State, Kentucky, Arizona, Dayton, Marquette and Illinois.[26] Howard was evaluated as the best senior at the camp that included Chris Webber, Cherokee Parks, Robinson, and Alan Henderson.[23][27] After being named Most Valuable Player (MVP) at the prestigious Boston Shootout and being acknowledged as the leading participant at Nike camp, he was mentioned as the best prospect in the country. Howard's top six possible schools were Michigan, Nevada-Las Vegas, Kentucky, Marquette, Arizona and Dayton.[23] After he was declared the best player at the Nike camp, his household was besieged by recruiters. Howard issued the following statement "Contact my coach. I do not want my grandmother and aunt upset about calls at all times of the day and night. I'm not the only person who lives in this house. I think my wishes should be respected. If not, when it comes time to make my decision, I'll take those things into account."[28] Although Howard was the best performer at the camp, some felt Webber and Robinson were his equals as prospects.[29] Howard was Illinois' number one recruit, but the team was under investigation for recruiting violations.[30] By the end of July 1990, Howard was projected to sign with Michigan.[31]

Senior year

In the final days before his senior season decision regarding his college basketball team decision, Howard wavered between Arizona State and Michigan. He had also made an official visit to Dayton.[24] Despite the ongoing recruiting scandal, he also made an unofficial visit to the University of Illinois, which had recruited four of the five previous Chicago Public School League Illinois Players of the Year.[32] At the time of his decision, he was considered one of the top five seniors in the country, but he decided not to hold a press conference to announce his decision.[33] Although the official signing period was set for November 14–21, 1990, he selected Michigan on November 2.[34] Howard averaged 26.9 points, 8.4 rebounds and 3.4 assists during his senior season,[2] and finished in the top 10 percent of his academic class.[35] He had become friends with Jimmy King when they visited Michigan on the same weekend and is said to have influenced King's decision to enroll there.[2][36] Howard's signing made up for the inability of Steve Fisher, Michigan's head coach, to recruit Eric Montross the prior year,[37] even though Montross' father and grandfather had played for Michigan.[38] With Jalen Rose, Webber, Howard and King, the Michigan recruiting class was considered to be the best in the nation.[39] Some regarded the class as among the greatest recruiting classes of all time.[40][41]

As a senior, Howard edged Griffith and Kiwane Garris for the most votes to the Sun-Times' annual All-Chicago Public School League boys' basketball team.[42] He was also selected to the All-Area team.[43] He was also a repeat Class-AA All-State selection.[44] Howard, Kleinschmidt and Robinson were all selected to the 10-member first team of Parade magazine's 40-member high school All-America boys' basketball team.[5] Howard and Kleinschmidt were selected to play in the McDonald's All-America game.[4] In the game, West MVP Webber posted 28 points and 12 rebounds and Howard added 16 points as the West won 108–106.[45] Howard also earned the Gatorade Circle of Champions' Illinois Player of the Year Award.[46] In late April, Howard was informed that his ACT test score was high enough to make him eligible under Proposition 48 academic requirements to play as a freshman.[47] Only eight of the top 25 Chicago Public School League players passed the test.[48]

[edit] College career

Freshman year
Michigan's Fab Five (left to right) Jimmy King, Jalen Rose, Chris Webber, Ray Jackson and Howard

As Michigan celebrated Midnight Madness on October 15, 1991, there was already talk that at least four of the five freshmen would be starting before the season ended.[49] (The five eventually started in a combined 304 of a possible 350 man-games among them during their first two seasons.)[1] Early in his freshman season for the 1991–92 Michigan Wolverines, Howard started in some games and came off the bench in others for the highly-rated Wolverines.[50] Over time, he won the starting role from Eric Riley.[51] In the first matchup against Illinois, Howard scored 13 points and denied Thomas the ball consistently enough to hold him to 8 shots. His biggest contribution in the game was getting his hand on a loose ball to force a jump ball with 16 seconds left and Michigan leading by only three.[52] In the Elite Eight round of the 1992 NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament, Michigan earned a rematch against a Jimmy Jackson-led Ohio State Buckeyes team that had beaten them twice during the regular season by double digits.[53] Michigan won the rematch, during which the Fab Five scored all but two Wolverines points.[54] When the 24–8 Wolverines reached the Final Four round of the tournament, they found themselves matched against a 29–4 Bob Huggins-coached Cincinnati Bearcats team that averaged 83.6 points per game and had lost to only three teams, two of which had beaten Michigan.[55] Nick Van Exel, who became the starter in the middle of the season, led Cincinnati in postseason scoring.[56] Howard, King and Riley shaved their heads for the game.[57] Michigan won and earned a rematch with the Duke Blue Devils team that had beaten them by three points in overtime in December.[2][58] In the initial contest, Howard had only scored four points.[2] Entering the final game, he was averaging 11.2 points and 6.3 rebounds.[2] The day before the game, Howard endured dehydration from stomach cramps and a fever that necessitated that he receive fluids. As a result of his condition he was exempted from mandatory media meetings.[59][60] Howard was part of a rotation with Webber and Riley that guarded the National Player of the Year, Christian Laettner.[2] During Laettner's first six possessions against Howard, Laettner dribbled the basketball off his foot, missed a shot, threw a turnover, traveled, threw the ball away and hit the backboard with a shot.[2] However, Duke scored in its final 12 possessions of the championship game, going on a 23–6 run to win by a final margin of 71–51.[61] Despite his medical difficulties, Howard contributed 9 points in 29 minutes.[59] Howard earned a reputation for his quick feet, ability to grasp fundamentals, excellent moves in the low post, but also 2-to-1 turnover-to-assist ratio and a high number of fouls.[2]

Sophomore year

As his sophomore year began, there were reports that during the 1992 summer a controversy had arisen when three Wolverines were found to have been paid $300 to participate in a charity basketball tournament. Furthermore, some others, including Howard, appeared at several summer basketball camps together, which was a possible violation of NCAA rules.[62] The following season, Michigan returned its top nine scorers and began the season ranked number one in the country by the Associated Press.[63] Michigan lost its second game of the season in a rematch with Duke.[64] As a sophomore member of the 1992–93 Wolverines, Howard was described as the steadiest player by coach Fisher.[65] Meanwhile, the Chicago vocational team that he led to back-to-back city Final Fours fell to successive 4–20 and 4–21 records without him.[66] During the season, Howard acquired a million-dollar disability insurance policy approved by the NCAA under the Exceptional Student Athlete Disability Insurance Program available to student-athletes who are projected to be chosen high in the NBA, National Football League (NFL), and Major League Baseball drafts.[67] As a freshman and sophomore, he was not only united with the Fab Five and future NBA player Riley, he was teammates with Rob Pelinka.[68] In the semifinals of the 1993 NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament against Kentucky, Howard held Jamal Mashburn in check defensively, while contributing on offense.[69][70] When the 31–4 Wolverines were matched up against the 33–4 1992–93 Tar Heels in the championship game, both coach Fisher and Carolina's coach Dean Smith were seeking their second national championship.[71] During the championship game Howard picked up his second personal foul with 9 minutes 42 seconds remaining in the first half as the entire team dealt with foul trouble.[72] The game would be remembered as the one in which Webber's technical foul for calling a time out when the team had none left led to a Tar Heel victory.[73][74] Over the course of the season, Howard averaged 14.6 points and 7.4 rebounds.[75] After the season, Webber and Howard were invited to try out for the United States national basketball team that would compete at the 1993 World University Games and Under-22 World Championships.[76]

Junior year

After Webber left for the NBA, the 1993–94 Wolverines team entered the season ranked fifth in the nation as it opened the season against number 13 Georgia Tech.[77] During the season, Howard had a case of the chicken pox in January.[78] Michigan found itself with a 21–6, (13–4 Big Ten) record and tied with the Purdue Boilermakers for the conference lead with one game remaining.[79] Michigan then lost (for the third time in its last four games) to a struggling Northwestern Wildcats team,[80] and finished second in the conference.[81] After the season, Howard was selected as a first team All-Big-Ten member along with Robinson, Rose, Shawn Respert, and Damon Bailey.[82] In the opening round of the 1994 NCAA Men's Division I Basketball Tournament, Howard helped Michigan survive with a 78–74 overtime victory over Pepperdine by scoring 28 points and adding 9 rebounds before fouling out.[83] In the second round, Howard posted 34 points and 18 rebounds to lead the team to a 84–79 victory over Texas.[84] Michigan faced a Joe Smith-led Maryland in the Sweet Sixteen round.[81] Howard scored 24 and had 11 rebounds before fouling out with 2:49 remaining in the 78–71 victory.[85] Howard earned the regional MVP award with a game-high 30 points and 13 rebounds despite collecting two fouls in the first two minutes in the Elite Eight round against a victorious Arkansas team that had United States President Bill Clinton as a vocal supporter.[86] After the season, he announced his intention to enter the 1994 NBA Draft.[87] The following day, Rose announced he would enter the draft as well.[88]

Howard left Michigan after being named an Associated Press third team All-American during his junior year, and was taken by the Washington Bullets fifth overall in the 1994 NBA Draft.[89] Howard was represented by David Falk.[90] Although the Fab Five games in the Final Four have been forfeited,[91] Howard was not among the players (including Robert Traylor, Webber, Rose, Maurice Taylor, and Louis Bullock) called before a grand jury to testify[92] in the University of Michigan basketball scandal and was not found to have received large amounts of money.[93]

[edit] Professional career

[edit] Scouting report

In college, Howard was regarded as one of the best defensive big men in the country.[51] He was also regarded as the rock-solid, rebounds-and-defense power forward in contrast to his flamboyant teammate Webber.[94] Michigan coach Steve Fisher referred to him as "My Rock of Gibraltar".[84] His consistency was described by a Chicago Tribune journalist poetically as "their ballast, steadying them on those many occasions when they wavered. And their savior, rescuing them from their many follies".[84]

After the 1994 NBA Draft, NBA analyst Doug Collins described Howard as a player who can "play with his back to the basket and can shoot from about 16 feet outside" and who "plays with a lot of energy and emotion".[15] The Baltimore Sun noted that he is a power forward who is able to play center, adding that at Michigan he was "most effective playing with his back to the basket" but could also pass effectively and hit medium-range jumpers.[95] The Bullets' head coach Jim Lynam described Howard as a "complete player" and noted that "he can defend you and he can score over you".[96] The team's general manager, John Nash, said that "the things that impressed me most about him were his character, his intelligence and his insight. He's a leader type."[97] Nash told another journalist that Howard was "as fundamentally sound as any player in the draft" and that he "has a discipline about his game and uses a high level of skill and technique". The Baltimore Sun said, "Not only can Howard post up, he passes effectively and is able to hit a jumper up to 17 feet," adding that he "also is an excellent position defender".[98]

When he first became a free agent in 1996, Howard was described as versatile enough to play all three front-line positions (small forward, power forward and center), and Michael Jordan loved his "game, work ethic and character".[99] In 2000–01 with Dallas, he was still regarded as a versatile offensive player who could "take advantage of smaller defenders in the paint and then stretch his bigger defenders outside" in addition to being a solid rebounder.[100] However by 2001, some regarded him as a high-priced, under-achieving player.[101] In 2002, he was described by a journalist from his hometown Chicago Sun-Times as a solid veteran at the four (power-forward position) but not a natural small forward.[102] In the NBA, Howard developed a respected inside post-up game and a reputation as a veteran leader, according to Kiki Vandeweghe, the general manager of the Denver Nuggets, in 2003.[103]

[edit] Early Washington era (1994–1996)

Entering the 1994–95 season, the NBA was without a collective bargaining agreement, and Howard was unsigned two weeks before training camp began. He continued to train in Chicago in late September while rumors swirled about possible trades.[90] On November 9, Carlos Rogers signed, making Howard the last first-round selection without a contract.[104] He missed most of the first month of the season and resided at an O'Hare Airport hotel while protracted talks continued.[105] The Bullets stuck to a 10-year, $30 million ($44.4 million in current dollar terms) offer.[106] On November 17,[107] Howard signed a 12-year, $37.5 million ($55.6 million) contract and eventually moved into the starting lineup.[105] While holding out, he was rumored to be part of a package deal with Calbert Cheaney and a first-round draft choice for Scottie Pippen.[105][108] Another rumor had Howard being packaged with Rex Chapman and Don MacLean for Pippen.[109] Once he signed, he was reunited with Michigan teammate Webber as a member of the Bullets.[110] Webber was traded from the Golden State Warriors to the Bullets on November 18 for Tom Gugliotta and three first-round draft choices.[107] Webber had also sat out training camp and the first eight games of the season.[111]

After Webber joined the Bullets, many thought that the two former Fab Five members would bring success to the team, coached by Lynam. The Washington front line was anticipated to include Webber, Howard, and Kevin Duckworth and to have Gheorghe Mureşan, a 7-foot-7-inch (231 cm) Romanian center coming off the bench.[111] Chapman, MacLean, Cheaney, Mitchell Butler and Scott Skiles were expected to provide solid perimeter play.[111] Experts projected the Bullets as contenders for the conference title.[111] As a power forward, Howard posted impressive numbers once he became a regular.[105] Over the course of the season, he averaged 17.0 points per game and posted 17 double-doubles in 65 games played (52 starts).[89][112] He totalled 30 points or more on three separate occasions.[112] Howard participated in the February 11, 1995, NBA All-Star Game weekend events as a member of the youth challenge match.[113] He earned second team All-Rookie honors at the end of the season,[89] and became the first NBA athlete who entered the draft early and still graduated with his academic class.[114] The team endured 317 man-games of injury, however,[115] and finished with a 21–61 record.[116]

He majored in radio-television communication (with a minor in business) at Michigan and graduated to fulfill his promise to his grandmother. Although had spent the prior year playing in the NBA, he returned to campus to partake in graduation ceremonies with his classmates.[117] Howard had promised his grandmother on the final day he saw her alive that he would graduate. When he made it to the NBA, he quickly realized how much leisure time the multimillionaire players had and decided to do something productive instead of find ways to spend his new riches. Howard stated that "I knew if I kept pushing it off, I'd never get it done." In order to complete his final 32 course hours, he took summer classes in 1994. Then, he continued to study on road trips and mailed in papers from the nearest post office. He took correspondence classes and independent study. While juggling professional basketball duties and coursework, he continued to find time for charity work and time to visit hospitals. In the end he earned his bachelor of arts degree. He feels it makes him a better example when he serves as a speaker about staying in school.[118] During the graduation, the keynote speaker, Marian Wright Edelman, paid special recognition to Howard and Fab Five teammates King and Jackson who graduated together and noted that Howard's graduation made him a role model for children.[119]

In the off-season, the Bullets traded Chapman, MacLean and Duckworth,[120][121][122] and they drafted Rasheed Wallace.[123] The team was expected to be a contender with Webber, Howard, Muresan, Mark Price, and Robert Pack. Webber, Price and Pack missed almost the entire 1995–96 season (65, 75 and 51 games respectively) because of injuries.[115] in spite of the injuries to several key teammates, 1995–96 was Howard's best season. During the season, he finished 3rd in the NBA in minutes played, 6th in points scored and 10th in points per game (22.1).[89] Over the course of the season, he accumulated 22 double-doubles in 81 games, including 6 in his last 8 games.[124] He concluded the season by scoring at least 20 points in his last 16 games.[124] His strong finish earned him NBA player of the month for April,[114] and he earned his only career All-Star game selection for the 1996 NBA All-Star Game and his only All-NBA selection at the end of the season.[89] Howard became just the second player in Washington franchise history, after Bernard King, to post back-to-back 40-point games (against Boston on April 17, 1996, with 40, and at Toronto on April 19, 1996, with 42).[125][126] In addition to his two 40-point performances, he posted 3 additional 30-point performances and scored at least 20 points in a total of 56 of 81 games.[124] Howard also made news that season for his theatrics after being ejected from a February 27 game against the Charlotte Hornets. He kicked a water cooler across the court and was fined $5,000 for making an obscene gesture.[127] The 42 points proved to be his career high.[128] Averaging 22.1 points, 8.1 rebounds and 4.4 assists,[128] he helped the Bullets record 39 victories.[124] This was not enough for the Bullets to make the playoffs during their ninth consecutive losing season.[129]

After averaging 17 points per game as a rookie and more than 22 per game in his second season, Howard became a free agent. The Bullets offered Howard an $89 million contract, and the Miami Heat outbid them with a seven-year deal estimated to be worth $98 to $101 million. However, the Heat miscalculated its available salary under the salary cap by excluding performance bonuses for Tim Hardaway and P. J. Brown and failing to account for the impact of renegotiating Alonzo Mourning's contract before coming to terms with Howard.[130] The league rejected the contract on July 31 because the $9 million for the 1996–97 season violated the cap.[130][131] Howard then re-signed with the Bullets on August 5. He became the first player in NBA history to sign a contract worth more than $100 million;[132][133] his seven-year contract was worth $105 million. He never reached the level of All-Star status again. The league ruled on August 5 that the Bullets could re-sign Howard after having renounced his rights on July 15 to free up cap room to sign Tracy Murray and Lorenzo Williams if they forfeited their 1997 NBA Draft first-round selection rights.[134] The Bullets were not limited by the cap because they were re-signing their own player.[131] The Heat pursued legal remedy in the Florida state courts seeking acknowledgment of the prior validity and superiority of their earlier contract.[134] Although the two contracts that Howard had signed seemed headed for arbitration, the Heat dropped its legal pursuits of Howard.[135] If the Heat had pursued arbitration and had been found guilty of violating the salary cap, the team could have been fined $5 million and Pat Riley could have been suspended for the season. By dropping their actions, the Heat avoided any possible penalties.[136] In addition to signing free agents Murray and Williams, the Bullets traded for Rod Strickland and Harvey Grant.[137]

[edit] Late Washington era (1996–2001)

The Bullets became the Washington Wizards after asking fans to vote on the name.[138] Over the course of the 1996–97 season, Howard accumulated 24 double doubles in 82 games,[139] while averaging 19.1 points and 8.1 rebounds.[128] Although Howard averaged over 19 points per game and played all 82 games,[128] he only scored 30 or more points twice during the regular season.[139] Howard's only NBA playoff appearance in his six plus seasons with the Washington franchise came during the 1997 NBA Playoffs, when he averaged 18.7 points and 6 rebounds while playing 43 minutes per game.[128] After Lynam coached the Bullets to a 22–24 record, the Bullets hired Bernie Bickerstaff, who posted a 22–13 record.[140] The team finished its regular season with a 44–38 record but was swept in three games in the Eastern Conference first round by the Chicago Bulls, who went on to win their second consecutive NBA championship.[141]

At the beginning of the 1997–98 season, the Wizards relocated from the US Airways Arena to the MCI Center.[142] Over the course of the 1997–98 season, Howard accumulated 11 double-doubles in 64 games,[143] while averaging 18.5 points and 8.0 rebounds.[128] He failed to score 30 points in any game.[89] During Bickerstaff's only full season as the Washington coach, the team posted a 42–40 record.[144] Howard, who injured his ankle,[145] played in no games between February 5 and March 17.[143] On April 6, 1998, a woman filed a sexual assault complaint against Howard and Webber (traded by the team a few weeks later) after a party at Howard's house. Lawyers for both offered different accounts; Howard's lawyer said that the woman had initiated an unspecified consensual sex act with one of the men. A grand jury heard testimony from the accuser and many of the 40 so party attendees but neither of the players before the Montgomery County State's Attorney announced that prosecution was not warranted and closed the case in May.[146] In June, Howard filed suit against the woman; he sought compensation for emotional distress for damage to his reputation and image.[147] Melissa Reed, the accuser, was absent during the proceeding and failed to respond to the lawsuit for defamation. In November, Howard was awarded $1 in compensatory damages and $100,000 in punitive damages plus legal costs.[148]

After the 1998–99 NBA lockout, the Wizards posted an 18–32 record. The team started out 13–19 under Bickerstaff and was 5–13 under Jim Brovelli.[149] Howard, a vocal detractor of Bickerstaff, said the coach was not able to make proper in-game adjustments.[150] Howard, who again had an ankle injury,[151] missed the last 14 games of the season. Over the course of the 1998–99 season, he accumulated 11 double-doubles and two 15-rebound performances in 36 games,[152] while averaging 18.9 points and 7.0 rebounds.[128]

During the 1999–2000 season, Howard accumulated 10 double-doubles in 82 games,[153] while averaging 14.9 points and 8.1 rebounds.[128] That season the Wizards endured a 14–30 start under head coach Gar Heard before going 15–23 under Darrell Walker.[154] By his sixth season, Howard had become unpopular and a bit of a disappointment in Washington, according to a journalist from the Chicago Tribune.[155] During the season, he twice posted 30 points,[153] including a season-high 36 in a fourth-quarter comeback to end a five-game losing streak in January.[156]

During the 2000–01 season, his fifth for the Wizards since last being named an All-Star, Howard's pay remained the fourth-highest in the league, behind Kevin Garnett, Shaquille O'Neal and Mourning.[157] On December 31, 2000, Howard, in a game against the Detroit Pistons, posted his career high of 15 free throws.[128] Michael Jordan, who had become part-owner of the team, traded Howard, Obinna Ekezie, and Calvin Booth to the Dallas Mavericks for Laettner, Loy Vaught, Etan Thomas, Hubert Davis, Courtney Alexander and $3 million on February 22, 2001, at the NBA trade deadline.[158][159] Jordan's move was praised for freeing up salary-cap space in advance of the NBA's first season with a luxury tax.[160] That season, Washington finished the year with a 19–63 record under coach Leonard Hamilton.[161]

[edit] 2001–2004

During the 2000–01 season, the Mavericks finished 53–29 under coach Don Nelson.[162] Howard provided the Mavericks with a back-to-the-basket player who moved into the starting power-forward position, enabling Dirk Nowitzki to play small forward and Bradley to play center.[100] During the season, Howard accumulated 16 double-doubles and five 30-point performances in 81 games,[163] while averaging 18.0 points and 7.1 rebounds.[128] On a team with All-star Michael Finley and future MVPs Steve Nash and Nowitzki, Howard was the highest paid player.[164] On March 20, Howard blocked five shots, his career high, against the Portland Trail Blazers.[128] The Mavericks advanced past the Utah Jazz 3–2 before falling to the San Antonio Spurs 4–1 in the 2001 NBA Playoffs.[162] The Utah games marked the only time Howard played for a team that won an NBA playoff series.[89] During the series against the Spurs, Howard slammed Spurs guard Derek Anderson to the floor, separating Anderson's shoulder, and was ejected.[165] In the Mavericks' 10 playoff games, Howard accumulated three double-doubles while averaging 13.4 points and 8.3 rebounds in 39.1 minutes.[89][163]

During the 2001–02 season, he accumulated 17 double-doubles and three 30-point performances in 81 games (72 starts),[166] while averaging 14.6 points and 7.6 rebounds.[89] This was the first time since his rookie holdout season that he did not start all his games played.[89] All non-starts occurred between November 21 and December 11, and eight of them were in consecutive games between November 21 and December 5.[166] On January 31, he posted a career-high 16 rebounds against the Houston Rockets.[128] The Mavericks traded him with Donnell Harvey, Tim Hardaway and a 2002 first-round pick to the Denver Nuggets for Raef LaFrentz, Avery Johnson, Nick Van Exel and Tariq Abdul-Wahad on February 21, 2002.[89][167] At the time of the trade, Howard was considered the Mavericks' best low-post defender.[168] Howard started all 28 games that he played for the Nuggets.[166] Don Nelson's Mavericks posted a 57–25 record and went to the second round of the playoffs after trading Howard,[169] while the Nuggets posted a 27–55 record under coaches Dan Issel and Mike Evans.[170] On March 25, 2002, he scored his 10,000th point.

During a preseason game, Howard attempted to punch Al Harrington and Jermaine O'Neal, which caused the NBA to suspend him on October 25, 2002. As a result, Howard missed the Nuggets' first two games of the regular season, and this cost him $458,000 in salary.[171] During the 2002–03 regular season, he accumulated 18 double-doubles, two 30-point performances, and three 15-rebound performances in 77 games,[172] while averaging 18.4 points and 7.6 rebounds.[89] The 2002–03 Nuggets were 17–65 under Jeff Bzdelik.[173] Howard started all 77 games in which he played.[172]

He then signed as a free agent with the Orlando Magic on July 16, 2003.[89] Over the course of the 2003–04 season he accumulated 16 double-doubles, and two 30-point performances in 81 games (77 starts),[174] while averaging 17.2 points and 7.1 rebounds.[89] The team compiled a 21–61 record, the worst in the NBA, under coaches Doc Rivers and Johnny Davis.[175][176]

[edit] Houston era (2004–2007)

Howard as a Houston Rocket

Right: Shooting over Antawn Jamison as Yao Ming looks on

On June 29, 2004, Howard and Magic teammates Tracy McGrady, Tyronn Lue and Reece Gaines were part of a seven-player trade that sent 2003–04 Houston Rockets starting guards Steve Francis and Cuttino Mobley plus Kelvin Cato to the Magic.[89][177] Over the course of the 2004–05 season Howard achieved seven double-doubles in 61 games (47 starts),[178] while averaging 9.6 points and 5.7 rebounds.[89] His 26.6 minutes per game marked the first season that he played less than 32 minutes per game.[89] Maurice Taylor started the first 16 games of the season for the Rockets.[179] Although Taylor was available and played,[180] Howard became the regular starter on December 2 and started for the rest of the season.[178] The 2004–05 Rockets were 51–31 under Jeff Van Gundy, and they lost in the first round of the 2005 NBA Playoffs to the Dallas Mavericks 4 games to 3.[181] Although the Rockets made the playoffs, Howard's season ended on March 14, when he left a game with a sprained right knee.[178][182] The injury coincided with a period in which he developed symptoms of viral myocarditis, including heart palpitations, mild chest pains and fever and was diagnosed with a viral infection in his heart. His limited physical activity impaired his medial collateral ligament rehabilitation.[183]

During the 2005–06 season, Howard recorded 10 double-doubles and two 30-point performances in 80 games,[184] while averaging 11.8 points and 6.7 rebounds.[89] He played 31.7 minutes per game, and this was the last season in which he averaged 30 minutes per game as well as the last time that he started at least half of the games in which he played.[89] The Rockets were 34–48 under Van Gundy and missed the playoffs.[185]

In the 2006–07 season, Howard achieved nine double-doubles in 80 games played (37 starts),[186] while averaging 9.7 points and 5.9 rebounds.[89] His 26.5 minutes per game marked a new career low.[89] The Rockets acquired Shane Battier, who started all 82 games.[187] Although Howard only started 38 games, he started 32 consecutive games between December 26, 2006, and March 3, 2007.[186] This streak coincided with Yao Ming's absence from the lineup.[188] The 2006–07 Rockets were 52–30 under Van Gundy and lost in the first round of the 2007 NBA Playoffs to the Utah Jazz, four games to three.[187] In the playoffs, Howard, coming off the bench, averaged 5.0 points and 4.4 rebounds in 22.4 minutes.[89][186] Although Howard was described as a consistent role player and locker-room leader,[189] his salary continued to be perceived as outrageous.[190]

[edit] 2007 and after

On June 14, 2007, Howard was traded to the Minnesota Timberwolves for Mike James and Justin Reed.[191] He was looking forward to playing with Kevin Garnett.[192] However, Garnett was traded to the Boston Celtics on July 31.[193] Howard made it clear he was not interested in being part of a youth movement in Minnesota and consequently requested a trade once Garnett had been dealt.[192][194] Howard still had $6.88 million and $7.38 million in salary owed to him over the next two seasons, which made him difficult to trade.[192] Wolves owner Glen Taylor noted that the team would attempt to accommodate his wishes but also noted that it would be difficult.[195] On October 29, 2007, the Timberwolves waived Howard after agreeing to a contractual buyout agreement[196] worth $10 million spread over four years instead of the roughly $14.25 million ($6.88 million plus $7.38 million) that Minnesota would otherwise have owed him.[197]

Howard signing autographs as a Dallas Maverick

Howard agreed to terms with the Dallas Mavericks on October 30, 2007; however he was not able to officially sign until the 31st, when he cleared waivers.[198][199] During the 2007–08 season, he played in 50 games and made no starts,[200] while averaging 1.1 points and 1.6 rebounds.[89] In his limited role he never played more than 18 minutes and had season-highs of seven rebounds and six points.[200] The 2007–08 Mavericks were 51–31 under coach Avery Johnson and lost in the first round of the 2008 NBA Playoffs to the New Orleans Hornets four games to one.[201] In the playoffs, Howard only appeared for a total of 11 minutes in three games.[89] This was the only season in Howard's career in which he did not start in a single game.[89]

On October 3, 2008,[89] Howard rejoined the Denver Nuggets, but was later released when the Nuggets made a three-for-one trade of Allen Iverson for Chauncey Billups, Antonio McDyess and Cheikh Samb on November 3.[202] When he was waived, he was the only player on the Nuggets' roster without a guaranteed contract.[203] Before the trade, Howard had played in three games during the 2008–09 season. His appearances had all come from off the bench and lasted between seven minutes and three seconds and seven minutes and forty-two seconds.[204] After Howard departed, the Nuggets went on to post a 54–28 record and reach the Western Conference finals under George Karl.[205] On December 12, 2008, he was signed by the Charlotte Bobcats.[206] During the 2008–09 season, he played in 42 games, making two starts,[204] while averaging 4.1 points and 1.8 rebounds.[89] In his role, he played more than 20 minutes five times including four in a row from January 28 to February 8 and had season-highs of five rebounds and 14 points. He posted 10 or more points five times.[204] He more than 20 minutes on January 28, the night after Gerald Wallace suffered a left-lung collapse as well as a non-displaced fracture of the fifth rib.[207] Although he played 20 minutes in four consecutive appearances, he sat out one intervening game on February 6 with a toe injury.[208] On February 8, in his only start other than his first game as a Bobcat, he played a season-high 30:39 and posted a season-high 14 points.[204] The subsequent night Howard was out of the lineup again with a toe injury.[209] The 2008–09 Bobcats were 35–47 under coach Larry Brown.[210]

Howard as a Portland Trail Blazer against Amar'e Stoudemire

On September 17, 2009, Juwan Howard signed a one-year deal with the Portland Trail Blazers.[211][212] During the 2009–10 season, he accumulated two double-doubles, played in 73 games, and made 27 starts,[213] while averaging 6.0 points and 4.6 rebounds.[128] On December 22, Joel Przybilla injured his knee.[214] That night Howard had his first double-double of the season.[213] He had his other double-double the next night when he made his first start of the season.[200] All of Howard's performances with 10 rebounds or more occurred between December 22 and February 3. December 23 marked the start of 14 consecutive starting appearances and 24 starts in 26 appearances.[213] The 2009–10 Trail Blazers were 50–32 under coach Nate McMillan and lost in the first round of the 2010 NBA Playoffs to the Phoenix Suns, four games to two.[215] Howard appeared in all six games, averaging 3.3 points and 2.7 rebounds in 14.5 minutes.[89]

On July 20, 2010, Howard came to terms with the Miami Heat for the veteran's minimum salary.[216][217] Although he will only be paid the minimum by the Heat, he is in the final year of his four-year buyout from the Timberwolves.[197]

[edit] Personal

Howard has remained a Chicago resident throughout his NBA career. In 1996, he bought a 3,116-square-foot (289.5 m2) town home in the South Loop area for $490,000 that he continues to own.[218] In 2009, he paid $2.55 million for a three-bedroom, 3,080-square-foot (286.1 m2) unit in the Trump International Hotel & Tower.[218] In 2005, Howard bought a 3.5-acre (14,000 m2) $11.775 million property in South Florida's Gables Estates, where he intended to build a 15,000-square-foot (1,400 m2) home, with a dock for a newly purchased 54-foot (16 m) yacht named the Fab 5.[219]

Howard's son, Juwan Howard, Jr. (born February 5, 1992),[220] is the child of Markita Blyden, who was runner-up for Michigan's Miss Basketball when she and her twin sister led Detroit's Murray Wright High School to the 1990 Class A state championship game. He finished his senior season at Detroit's Pershing High School in Spring 2010.[221] As a junior, he led his high school to the Michigan High School Athletic Association state championship. As a senior, he was named first team All-State, by both the Associated Press and Detroit Free Press.[220] He committed to play for the Western Michigan Broncos men's basketball team.[221][222]

On July 6, 2002, Howard married Jenine Wardally. At the time, Howard was 29 and Wardally was 27. They have a son Jace who was born in late September 2001.[223][224] They have a second son Jett, who is two years younger.[225]

[edit] Other endeavors

Howard is active in fostering youth basketball activities through the Juwan Howard Foundation, which helps underprivileged youth in Washington, DC and Chicago. He has been recognized as one of the "Good Guys in Sports" by The Sporting News for his civic contributions.[226] In April 2010, Howard won the NBA Cares Community Assist Award for his community efforts, philanthropic work and charitable contributions.[227] His foundation partners with the Chicago Public Schools (CPS) to reach 30,000 kids annually for a reading challenge, where the top 300 readers get to attend his basketball camp.[227] By partnering with the Juwan Howard Foundation, CPS, Jordan Brand, Dell Computers, EMI Music, Vitamin Water and the National Basketball Association, he is able to maintain the camp at no fee to the kids.[228]

[edit] Pop culture

Howard had a small role in The West Wing, appearing in the 1999 episode "The Crackpots and These Women" as Mr. Grant,[229] a former college basketball player working on the President's Council on Physical Fitness. Grant joins a pick-up basketball game on the side of the fictional President Josiah Bartlet, whose team appears to be losing to members of the President's staff. It is later revealed that Grant wins the game for the President.[230]

Howard also appeared in the 1994 basketball film Hoop Dreams.[231] Other appearances include the August 15, 1999 "The Art of Give and Take" episode of Arli$$, the season 5 (2005) "Michigan's Fab 5" episode of Beyond the Glory as well as the November 9, 1996 season 2 "Son-in-Law" episode of Hang Time.[232][233]
