Best Japanese in Pittsburgh, SWPA, maybe the whole East Coast
by PittsburghDiner (2 reviews)
August 26, 2009 - Likes it
Having lived in Japan for three years, I can say that Chaya is an authentic Japanese experience. Other places boast of "Japanese" cuisine, and then you find a chef standing over a hot piece of metal tossing his knives in the air, something you would never see in Japan. That is not Japanese food. Most Japanese people don't even know what a "hibachi" is. Chaya is Japanese food. Noodles, cutlets, tempuras, and well-prepared salads add another, delicious dimension to the incredible sushi menu. It gets crowded sometimes, even on weeknights, but it's entirely worth it. The friendly staff also works hard to make even busy times relaxing for everyone. If you've never tried sushi or you're an old pro, or if you want a real taste of Japan, Chaya is the place to go.

A mini-Kaiseki is an experience
by Louis (15 reviews)
July 30, 2009 - Likes it
[a 2006 blog entry]
I finally came back here to try the Kaiseki. Kaiseki is a tasting menu version of Japanese cuisine. Sashimi, teriyaki, tempura and sushi. But as good as the food is, the presentation is over the top. Each element served individually. And it is presented as a prix fixe meal, the chef picks what each element will be. And each element is presented in an elegant fashion, with the actual dish it is served on as well as the garnishing chosen to go with the food. Where it is offered, it gives the sushi chef a chance to show off. Last time I came here, I was with someone who only liked sushi so-so, so I wanted to come back again some day with someone who could appreciate it.
When we ordered the Mini-Kaiseki, the hostess asked if we had this before. When we replied that we had not, she started to explain it, then she gave up and called the chef over to describe what we were getting. The first of many signs that we were in for an experience.
The appetizer was eel and cucumber. The eel was very tender. Sashimi was tuna, salmon and shrimp. I was marveling that the sashimi was cut against the grain, so that the slices would fall apart in the mouth. The teriyaki was yellowtail.
A variety of tempura. For sushi it was again tuna, salmon and shrimp. Each element was served on a dish that complemented the food and the bed of garnish that it was on, so both sight and taste were given a feast.
It has been over a year since I came here, and my memories were exceeded by the experience this time around. Given a chance to show off, the chef and staff did good.

LOVE it!
by skimskim (5 reviews)
January 04, 2010 - Likes it
One of my top three favorite restaurants in Pittsburgh so far. I've been here four times now, and the sushi has always been fresh and delicious. They give very generous cuts of fish, which is crucial, since the bill ends up being pretty high.
I'm a big fan of their udon and beef negimaki. Also, you can't go wrong with soba if you're a fan, and the green tea noodles are a personal favorite.
The staff is always energetic and friendly. They do have a tendency to rush you, but it's understandable since the restaurant is immensely popular, and the lines get LONG. I always eat dinner early (6PM), however, and have never had to wait.
They recently changed locations, out of the tiny space they had down the street, and have more ample seating now. DEFINITELY worth at least one visit.

Salmon and Avocado Maki Roll
Mixed Maki Roll
Spicy Tuna Maki Roll
BIg Eye Tuna Nigiri Sushi
Seaweed Salad
Green Tea

recent visit was very good
by Joy (5 reviews)
November 14, 2009 - Likes it
I came here the week after it opened, to see what the buzz was about, and was underwhelmed. It was authentic, but not particularly impressive. Recently, I went back with two friends who wanted a sushi boat. At some restaurants, a sushi boat is used as an excuse to dump some not-very-impressive fish (in some saggy, pre-made roles) onto customers who don't care enough to order specific items. Not here. This was fantastically fresh, amply-cut fish, and clearly freshly made rolls. I'm a fan now, for sure. We did eat enthusiastically and leave in an hour or so, so I can't address whether the service might have been less friendly if we'd chosen to linger. I'd suggest that if you plan to really linger, you not choose a very small, reasonably-priced restaurant (they need the table turnover). Or at least, come on a day that's not too busy, or order a sizable meal. We found the waitress very sweet and helpful when we had questions about specific fish or even unrelated cultural questions.

Too legit to quit.
by Barbara (9 reviews)
February 12, 2009 - Likes it
Most sushi joints I've encountered have not been run by Japanese people. That's okay - I'm fine with that. The fish is usually tastefully prepared, the soup is decent, and hopefully the price is right. However, Chaya is completely different. From what I've read, the chef used to cook in Japan, then went to New York City, and now has landed in his little Murray Avenue restaurant. What can I say other than lucky us? The food is superb and completely legitimate. I haven't found rice balls or an omakase dinner anywhere else (granted my budget usually wouldn't bring me to those places usually). Check this place out, but go early with not a lot of people because it's tiny and fills up quickly.
