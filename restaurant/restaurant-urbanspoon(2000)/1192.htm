Bad Experience at 'Aya Sofia'
by oceanwind (1 review)
February 23, 2009 - Doesn't like it
I, my wife, my brother and his wife visited a Turkish restaurant, Aya Sofia, after 5 years, on November 23rd, 2008, I belive. We all have lived in Turkey until 2003, before we came to the U.S. as graduate students. At first everything looked normal, except for the fact that there was no one to guide us into our table for about 5 minutes. We immediately started to order a lot of Turkish food which we have been missing so much: Calamari, Iskender, Lamb, Chicken Soup, etc. Immediately after we took our first bites however, everyone was looking at each other with the same expression on the face: Wait.. This is not Turkish food. And even worse, this is not good food!
They had identical grocery-store-sauce for both Calamari and Iskender for instance. Meat of Iskender was definitely not fresh and apparently was made of canned meat. But let alone not resembling Turkish food remotely, which Aya Sofia clams to be about, they were of very poor quality. They simply tasted bad. When, for example, my brother ordered Lamb he asked the waitress for "well done", but the one he got was literally dripping blood after the first cut. Airan, a Turkish drink, too tasted un-Turkish and simply bad.
The only exception was Chicken Soup, which was OK/edible. And the tip we paid to the waitress was probably less than she deserved, $8; she seemed to be a nice person and served us well. Aya Sofia is expensive, and for a modest dinner we paid about 82 dollars (plus 8 dollars of tip). To be honest, we were too shy to complain on the spot; we felt we were in our bother's residence after 5 years, and it would have been inconvenient to do so. But still, while leaving I told the waitress that we are from Turkey, and that what we had was not Turkish food. She replied apologetically that 'yes, today our Turkish chief is not the one who is cooking'. When I e-mailed the chief and the owner of the restaurant a day after, Mehmet Yildiz, complaining about the situation, he replied with a rather rude e-mail (for instance, one of his sentences was that 'if I was looking for a meal made by mom I was in the wrong place), and refused to accept any responsibility.
I feel they cheated us in this restaurant that misleads people and does not live up to minimal expectations of a second-rate dining place, while charging the price of the first-rate one. I am extremely angry that they play with innocent people's lack of information about true Turkish food, and even fail to serve food that simply tastes OK. Except for the price of Chicken Soup and the tip for the waitress we totally deserved reimbursement. Of course, the owner of Aya Sofia did not even bother to reply.

Is this what good Turkish food tastes like?
by South City Resident (1 review)
February 20, 2009 - Doesn't like it
My wife and I have driven by Aya Sofia several times. After looking at some of the wonderful reviews from others we decided to give it a try. This was our first experience at a Turkish restaurant.
When we arrived at the restaurant was decorated very pretty and it was very clean. During our meal our service was above average, but not excellent.
Since it was our first time to a Turkish place, we asked for recommendations from our server and she gave us several suggestions, and we decided to follow her advice. We ordered bocha, sarma, Izgara Kofte, and Sis Kebab. Prior to the hot plates arriving our server brought out pita bread with a tomato chutney. The tomato chutney was excellent. It had a wonderful flavor with just the right amount of spice. However, the pita bread was subpar. Many of the pieces were burnt in our basket.
Within a few minutes the bocha and sarma arrived. The bocha was cooked perfectly with a flaky filo dough outside, but the ground beef had a was dry and the mix of seasoning isn't taste good. The sarma was served with burnt grape leaves and again the ground beef mixture wasn't good. Maybe the grape leaves are suppose to be burnt, but if so, they shouldn't serve it. These two hot plates made us nervous about dinner.
With a reasonable amount of time, our entrees arrived. My wife ordered beef sis kebab and we figured it would be hard to screw these up. They should be good. My wife ordered them medium and they arrived medium on the inside, but totally burnt on the outside. I ordered izgara kofte after it was recommended by our server. The presentation wasn't pretty with what appered to be 6 hockey pucks on one side of the plate, a helping of rice and a vegatable. The izgara kofte again was burnt and charred like the other dishes. It came with a yogurt sauce that helped choke down a couple of them, but after two of them it was too much. I couldn't eat anymore.
Overall, it is a cute restaurant with terrible food in my opinion. The only saving grace was we went to Lion's Choice afterward and got a $.29 small dip cone. We should of saved our money and just went there for the whole meal.

I really really wanted to like Aya Sofia.  The owners are very nice, the atmosphere is terrific, but...  While I enjoyed some of the food - the cigara borayi (know I butchered that spelling) in particular - I had salmon there twice.  Once it was good.  The other it was obviously really old.  Really old.  And then there's my pet peeve - costly tiny glasses of wine.  For $8.50 to 11 dollars a glass, I better get a proper size pour of red wine in a proper red wine glass.  And the wine shouldn't have been open for days either.   I understand the large mark up in wine prices in all restaurants, but there is a fine line between mark up and highway robbery.   All the more the shame considering the overall niceness of  the staff, owners and atmosphere - especially in the bar, where I would love to go in the evening for a couple glasses of wine.  But who can enjoy that when you feel like you're being ripped off?
