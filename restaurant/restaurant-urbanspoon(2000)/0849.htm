The Applebee's of Tapas Restaurants
by Leopold Stotch (1 review)
February 08, 2009 - Doesn't like it
My friends and I were very excited to see OKC finally get a tapas restaurant so we tried out Bolero on a Saturday night. Sadly, Bolero was a big disappointment.
First, the atmosphere was not impressive. The restaurant portion of Bolero has a moveable window wall that was opened, and our table was near the sidewalk filled with people walking by. So we dined to the sound of heards of tweens coming and going from the nearby movie theater, and the smell of smoke wafting into the room. There was also a walkway in between the restaurant and bar portion of Bolero where two large groups were seated and there were children riding their bikes in between the tables and out onto the outside walkway. I'd expect that at Chuck E Cheese, but not at a restaurant where a filet mignon goes for $40.
The drinks were also a big disappointment. The margaritas were very weak and tasted like watered down sweet and sour mix. The sangria was nothing like what you may have tried in Florida or other Spanish restaurants; Bolero's pitcher of sangria was literally red wine poured over melting ice with some sliced oranges thrown in to make it look like sangria.
The menu was uninformative & limited. We had no idea what kinds of sauces, accents, etc came with the tapas we ordered and didn't realize that many of the things we ordered were dipped in batter and fried. The tapas offerings were skewed heavily towards chorizo and seafood (always a toss of the dice in the midwest in my opinion) with hardly any vegetarian options or general crowd-pleasing options like chicken, pork, or beef. We asked our server about portion size to get an idea about how many things we should order and she responded with information that did not answer the question asked.
I ordered the green salad which was described on the menu as mixed greens with purple onions and tomatoes, but when it came to the table, it turned out to be a bed of chopped romaine - straight out of a Fresh Express make-it-at-home salad bag - topped with 1oz of chopped purple onions and one cherry tomato sliced in half and placed on opposite ends of my plate. And the whole thing was just doused in oil. The crostinis that came with our olive tapenade were also soaked in a revolting amount of oil.
The food was pretty foul. Most of it was cold when it arrived and nothing was what we expected. With the exception of our scallops (served in a 1/4 inch of melted garlic butter), everything was fried and/or had a fried item on the plate which was an unwelcome surprise. All of the sauces and garnishes were off in one way or another - too bland, too oily, too salty, and so forth. Our group's collective opinion was that everything tasted like "fair food," i.e. foods that could be good but they've been dipped in batter and deep fried so that all you actually taste is oil and batter. The portions were entirely too large for what one would expect and we ended up with a good amount of uneaten food. Not surprisingly, no one wanted to take home the leftovers, or try out the desserts.
Cost (with drinks and tip) ended up being approximately $35/person. This was the worst meal I think I've ever had at that price point.
I won't go back and I wouldn't bother wasting my time or money at Bolero in OKC. Tapas, Spanish food and sangria will continue to be something that I enjoy as a treat when visiting large cities where, unlike Oklahoma City, a restaurant actually has to have good food and service to survive and get good reviews.
Oh, and if you do decide to visit Bolero don't bother trying to look at the (worthless) menu online beforehand- you'll just get directed to a page telling you that feature is "Comming Soon."

Relaxed Atmopshere in Bricktown
by Deb (14 reviews)
April 30, 2010
Went to Bolero with my husband last night for some pre-movie dinner and drinks. On a Thursday night, the restaurant was not at all crowded and had a very laid back atmosphere, like we were sitting on someone's patio. We ordered four different items off the tapas menu: fried goat cheese drizzled with honey, fried artichoke served with a cilantro oil and garlic aioli, chorizo and potato empanadas and serrano wrapped prawns. The food arrived in ten minutes, and while everything was very tasty, a combination of the same three sauces accompanied every dish. This may be the standard for a tapas menu, but to me, it made everything taste vaguely similar and I had hoped for more variety. We aslo sampled both the red and white sangrias, which were delicious, fruity and fresh. The sangria alone is worth coming back for.
