Magnifique!!
by Stinky (196 reviews)
February 24, 2010 - Really likes it
My true love of food was originally developed by my weekend trips to Paris while stationed in Germany some years ago. I can almost remember every meal I ever had over there. Rarely do we get to say that about a meal here at home, so since then, I have endeavored to relive some of those moments and Brasserie Pavil has done just that. As soon as I walked in, I was back in Paris in one of the big hotel Brasseries. A true brasserie in the grand Parisian scale, with true French Provincial dishes, the meal I had here was amazing, which started with Escargots in Puff pastry accompanied by a Chateau Famaey Malbec, and Prince Edward Island mussel's in white wine sauce. All wonderful. I then had one of my favorites sandwiches on earth, a Croque-Madame, which is a grilled ham & Gruyeres cheese with a fried egg and Mornay Sauce. Thats right - a grilled ham & cheese. Just Unbelievable!!! All the classics are here - Coq Au Vin, Cognac Pepper Steak, Bouillabaisse, Steak Au Poivre, Frog Legs, an amazing Foie Gras selection, and Cassoulet just to mention a few. Prices will run just a little high here as these dishes are extensive, intricately assembled works of true French cooking (that some call "Fussy") so you wont find a simple 12 dollar steak here (Thats what Golden Corral is for. Good luck with that.), but the quality, originality, and flavor presented make it well worth it. Seating is spacious and service the 2 times I've been have been 5 star. Take the time to discuss the extensive French wine list with your server or ask for the Sommelier. It will make the difference in your meal. I plan to go here as often as I can to try each and every dish on the menu. I'm sure my response will be "Magnifique!" every time.

Skip It
by Brent Byers (97 reviews)
May 29, 2010 - Doesn't like it
If you enjoy inauthentic French in contrived surroundings, poorly seasoned and prepared sauces, tasteless meats and shockingly awful frites then by all means go to Brasserie Pavil right now. You will get exactly what you want.
However, if you prefer sauces that do not taste like tomato mixed with burned meat, flavorful dishes that are seasoned with something other than salt and crisp, tender frites please go somewhere else.
So, the question is what did I have?
To start, the PEI Mussels in white wine sauce with fine herbs and chopped tomatoes. First, not all mussels come from Prince Edward Island. I have doubts that these did. The mussels smelled like fish. That is an undesirable quality in mussels. It is an indication of less than fresh mussels. Second, the mussels were sandy; an indication of improperly cleaned and prepared mussels. And the "fine herbs"? The overriding flavor was salt. Salt is an indication that flaws in the quality of ingredient or preparation is being hidden. The only herb present was parsley.
Next was the Pavil hanger steak with red wine shallot sauce. The steak wasn't terrible, it was only terribly prepared. If it had been "simply grilled with kosher salt" as the menu advertised the steak would have been fine. The problem was the red wine shallot sauce. The sauce was a take on bordelaise; however, very, very, very poorly executed. Either the marrow bones used to prepare the demi-glace were burned when they were roasted or the sauce relied on a canned or jarred demi-glace. In any event, the "red wine shallot sauce" tasted of burned meat mixed with tomato and salt instead of the concentrated beef, earth, and shallot that it should have. The primary flavor in the sauce was, again, salt.
And now to the frites. The surface of the "French fries" was translucent. They had either bathed in under temperature fat or they were reheated, previously frozen fries. The interior of the frites was mealy, lending credence to my belief they were not fresh cut and were, in fact, previously froze, defrosted, fries that had been heated in to often used, under temperature oil.
I know this restaurant took a significant amount of money to develop, design and build. But don't be taken in by that. Demand quality and don't be blinded by how much the building cost. After all, are you going to eat the real estate or the food?

Underwhelming
by Jeff Kleck (7 reviews)
December 10, 2009 - Doesn't like it
After eating at Roaring Fork last Friday, I saw Brasserie Pavil and wanted to give it a try. My opportunity came sooner than expected as I dined there this past Monday with some co-workers.
First impression is very nice as the main dining room has an open feel similar to Maggiano's main dining room. The ceiling must be 20 feet. The mirrors only add to the openness created by the high ceilings and abundant square footage.
Although the initial impression was favorable, it didn't take long for things to take a turn for the (gasp) worse. First concern was the music. For a restaurant trying to make a name as a French bistro, the music choice is odd, at best. 70s - 80s music is great, but it didn't fit here.
Second concern was our bread (I'm known in my family as the Pillsbury Doughboy). I like a crisp crust and a soft center, but the crust was too crisp - it was flat out HARD.
Third issue was the menu selection. Nothing looked so great that I had to have it. I ended up ordering the Ahi Tartare as my main course. I guess I was following the theme set by the calamari (which was very good, by the way!). The Ahi was ok, but not something I would order again.
The final straw was the desserts. They were out of the dessert one co-worker wanted, the crepes ordered by another were bland, and the highly touted carrot cake looked impressive, but was not nearly as good as advertised. It's a shame we wasted those calories.
There are positives. The French Onion Soup was a delicious and balanced blend of bread, cheese, onion, and broth. Calamari is tricky in that many restaurants serve a chewy rendition leaving you wanting something else. Here, that's not the case. It was crispy and tender with a flavor that had us ordering a second time.
Overall, we left unimpressed. Although I wouldn't turn down an offer to eat there (maybe I should give it another shot), I won't go out of my way to try it again.

Honestly, Don't Go
by Francis (2 reviews)
October 07, 2009 - Doesn't like it
Hello all,
I have been there four times with friends, with only one those visits legitimately being worth my while. The other three, however, showed glimmers of excellence but ultimately failed to meet expectations, and I (and the friends I go with) will not be making a fifth visit.
I am a major foodie and always visit restaurants multiple times; I will attempt to write an exhaustive summation of my visits.
Food
Pros:
The steaks are top notch; the steak au poivre is delicious, as is the bone-in-ribeye (which unfortunately was not on the menu on my last visit). In addition, the coq au vin is fantastic. French fries are fine, although nothing really discerns them from any good batch of fries you can get at a great burger joint. The scallops are wonderful, as is the calamari salad, and the duck confit is serviceable (a little more on it later). In addition, the cassoulet is good, as is their lamb shank. Oh, and the frito misto appetizer is sublime. To add, the bread is crusty, soft in the middle, and holds up to its purported "best in Sa' honorific. For dessert, their sundae is tasty, and their chocolate gateau is good as well.
Cons:
Their vegetable cassoulet is disgusting. A friend of mine ordered it (the day I ordered the regular cassoulet), and it basically tastes like mush; she ate about of a third of the dish. Their french onion soup is basically a gob of cheese; The server mentioned the inclusion of apple liquor or something along those lines layered into the dish to give it a distinctive flavor. It would have been nice to taste, however, I was unable to do so as there was a pound of melted cheese covering the meager amounts of actual soup.
The duck confit tastes great.... except for the salad part. Its basically frisee with absolutely nothing else. When I order duck confit, I would much rather have it as an entree along with vegetables and a starch (light, mashed potatoes perhaps). The duck confit salad at BP was sorely lacking, and had it been coupled with a substantial amount of greens and other vegetables, it would have been worthwhile. However, as is, the dish is severely lacking.
Last time we were there we had the risotto for an appetizer; tastes okay.... except its incredibly expensive compared the portion. If it was a better risotto, I wouldn't have a problem with the price.... but its not, and the price compared to quality is unjustifiable. Do not waste your time on the moules (mussels). Broth is bland, and the size of the mussels used are of the incredibly small variety (very small, in fact).
In addition, steer clear from their chilled jumbo shrimp. One can do that for MUCH cheaper and of higher quality simply at home by going to HEB and buying a pound of their chilled shrimp. No joke. Their chicken wings (looking at the menu online right now- called chicken lollipops) are decent, except again, you might as well go to a place like wingzone or wingstop, as the wings there taste much better! Really not worth it (especially due to their price).
In addition, we ALMOST ordered the seafood platter; that was until we saw it brought out to a table near us. For the price, it honestly is NOT worth it, as again, the jumbo shrimp is sub par, and the portions weren't in line with the price (almost $75?!). Also, I observed that the lobster barely had any lobster meat on it.
The burgers taste like a burger (one of my friend's ordered one), except if you are in the mood for one, opt for the infinitely better Bigz Burger Joint which is just down the street.
Another qualm- their cheese plate; we once ordered the cheese plate as an appetizer. Firstly, the cheese is sitting out on their bread stand (we were seated in area where we could observe the bread stand). Rather than having a chef in the back prepare a cheese plate, the servers do it themselves; as I am a little concerned about sanitation, as I noticed that the cheese is exactly at the height of our servers mouth (most people working there, we continually observed, were of the same height), meaning that ANYONE who stood in front of the bread stand basically breathes on the cheese. As for the quality, cheeses were good.... except you get the smallest slivers of cheese, getting more of the nuts, honey, etc. than the cheese themselves. And for 12.95? Again, I could go to central market and buy slivers of each cheese that would be larger, and the price would be less than the almost 14 (plus tax) we spent on it.
Service/Staff:
Servers and the other employees are nice and amiable. That said, one of the servers was very young, seemed like he had trouble talking to all of us, fumbling over his words in a rather meek manner, and was hard to hear. Just seemed way to shy, a little shakey on the menu, and could not give any wine recommendations. Another server was incredibly loud and overbearing; she talked a mile a minute, pushing products on us numerous times (inquiring over and and over throughout if we wanted alcoholic drinks, bottles of wine, etc), and while nice, was just too aggravating. In addition, another server we had literally laughed at everything we said (I am sure to seem endearing), and it quite possibly was the most distracting thing I can think of that can happen while eating. A few of the tables around us even looked over to look at what she was laughing at.
The other server (do not remember his name), was fine, capturing the balance between being helpful and knowledgeable and allowing us our space.
As for the managers, steer clear from the one named Luchiano (spelling?). Apparently the head manager? To us, he was incredibly cloying, and exuded false concern and interest. Like the server previously mentioned who was overbearing, same can be said for him. We had a couple of problems with the food once, and credit to him, he did drop by to make sure that things were alright. Other than that, he seemed to ignore all other tables, except for paying an undue amount of attention to a table near us, stopping by throughout the evening, bringing them extra food, and even escorting them out (we arrived there at about the same time as them, and were privy to observing this behavior). Despite him being up at the front when we left the restaurant, he did not even say a word, and the hostess was the only one to say goodbye. They must have been an important customer. There is another manager, an older gentleman, who is incredibly nice, and the sommelier is very very knowledgeable about wines; every recommendation he made for us paired nicely with our meals.
Decor:
When you first walk into Brasserie Pavil, it is instantly obvious that the owners have spared no expense on the restaurant's decor. From the large chandeliers, 'antiquated' mirrors (look like things from Pier 1) to the brass accouterments, BP is beautiful, but has a sheen of 'be impressed' as opposed to one of inviting intimacy and comfortability. Everything seems like a forced, expensive attempt at creation rather than one stemming from ingenuity or inspiration. Although this is rather snobby for me to observe, I would have no problems with it if the food, servers, and managerial staff were up to snuff. However, its not the case.
In sum, there are some good things on the menu. However, when viewing the place as a whole, with their prices, lack of intimate feel, sniff of store-bought snobbery, dismissive manager, and up and down menu (good items on the menu are TERRIFIC, bad are just bad), I would steer clear of here if I were you. I'll stick to other restaurants in the area, and for some other good french fare, check out Soleil (not as extensive a menu, but absolute delicious food and has the intimacy Brasserie Pavil sorely lacks, and a great wine menu to boot).
