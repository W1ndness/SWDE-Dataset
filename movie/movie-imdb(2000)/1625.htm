Storyline

Chris embarks on an odyssey of self-discovery that spans the globe. Kidnapped and enslaved by gun smugglers, sold by pirates and thrust into the murky underworld of gambling and kickboxing, Chris' journey takes him to forbidding Muay Thai Island where deadly martial arts are taught, the colonial splendor of British East Asia, the dank back alleys of Bangkok, desolate deserts once trod by the warriors of Genghis Khan and finally, the ancient Lost City. There he must face the ultimate test of his manhood in the fabled Ghang-gheng, the ancient winner-take-all competition in which the deadliest fighters from around the world employ the most spectacular feats of martial arts skills ever displayed in order to win the prized Golden Dragon. But fighting prowess alone will not be enough for Chris to triumph over such daunting foes. He must reach deep inside and access all of the determination, strength... User Reviews

 
VD offers no surprises in this forgettable but serviceable period piece adventure
12 May 2007 | by davideo-2 (United Kingdom) – See all my reviews

STAR RATING: ***** Saturday Night **** Friday Night *** Friday Morning ** Sunday Night * Monday Morning

Chris Dubois (Van Damme) is a street thief in the 1920s who is forced to steal on the streets with his group of street orphans. But when the cops close in, he is forced to go on the run and lands in a ship owned by a group of smugglers. He wakes up and is forced to work as a slave for the unscrupulous tyrants, who leave him for dead when things go wrong. But he is rescued by Lord Hobbs (Roger Moore) a gentleman pirate who ends up selling him to a man who trains Muay Thai fighters. Here he learns fighting skills that enable him to compete in the Ghen-Gheng, a renowned fighting contest held in Asia where fighters from around the world fight for the chance to win the Golden Dragon statue. But Hobbs returns on the scene and concocts a plan to steal the prized statue- a plan that leaves Chris not only fighting for his own life but the lives of those who have become dear friends.

Yay!-complete at last. I can now claim to have reviewed all of VD's movies up to this point, with the exception of Monaco Forever (and I really don't want to watch gay porn!) I finish with his first (and up to this point, only) directorial effort, a lavish but unoriginal and slightly ropey effort not regarded by fans as one of his most enjoyable films.

Many action faces like to try their hand at directing and starring in their own films, from Clint Eastwood, to Sly Stallone and even Steven Seagal, but they usually like to add an element of originality to their work (or, in Seagal's case, a social message!) But VD's work is so unremarkable simply because it's nothing new- it's a carbon copy of the plots from earlier films he did like A.W.O.L. and Bloodsport, with a ropey 1920s setting, employing not overly convincing period costumes and production design, with fight scenes that don't rank among his best either.

But it all just manages to be distracting enough, a corny but enjoyable ride with VD trying his best to do what he does best. **
